LGBT+ PEOPLE IN THE MEDIA

so, LGBT+ people in the media. this is both a very big issue, and a very small issue at the same time. while gay people in the media are pretty well represented, the media still fails to provide a full and accurate representation of the members of the LGBT+ community, as well as the variety of types of people in the LGBT community. for example. think of a lesbian shown in the media. that's easy, right? we've got hannah hart and ellen for one. and if you're like me you can think of lexa, and alex danvers, and every single character that katie mcgrath has ever played. but, i have a question for you, what do all of these women have in common? answer? they're all somewhat butch, and they're all white.

moving on, think of gay men in the media, if you're like me you can name a plethora of them who break the stereotypes as you watch star trek on repeat. but most people will probably hit will and grace, that kid from glee, and neil patrick harrison. and i'm going to ask you to name the common thread between all thse men. have you picked up on it again? for the most part they're all white, and they're all very obviously gay. okay.

now one more experiment. name a fictional transgender character. are you still trying to think of one? don't worry. i can only come up with lord fanny and alysia yeoh. both of whom are obscure comic book characters. beneath all of this lay a lot of issues in lgbt+ media representation that you ought to be aware of before you have sex, or come out. let's start with the obvious one.

the overview

so media really affects how we look at ourselves, and how other people look at us. media can help shape, or change, your identity and your opinions about yourself. and the media's influence on the lbgt+ community can be no less understated. while there are plenty of lgbt+ characters in the media, rarely are they a main character, and only very recently has that been getting better. but for years before gay characters were meant as jokes and people to be laughed at and made fun of. a large part of this reason has been because of the media.

so what makes up the media? well first of all? books, tv shows, news papers, movies, and basically anyway you can get information from. historically societies haven't been tollerate of lgbt+ people, and this has led to the media often disparaging which then led to the people disliking them even more, creating a somewhat endless cycle. lgbt+ people have been persecuted as criminals, labeled mentally ill and more. all of this stems from a so called 'normal' which has bee established by the poeple. this idea of normal is supposed to reflect what's 'right' often meaning a heterosexual couple in a nuclear family. it is, however, because of these opinions, that being lgbt+ still has such a stigma about it. the beliefs, policies, and media representation all stems from the idea that heteronormativity is 'normal' and therefore 'right' attatching a stigma to being lgbt+ as the people also believe that it's the 'right' and 'better' way to be.

it's because of this that we've shapped our beliefs of lgbt+ people. we've grouped ourselves into the binaries of male and female, and that straight is right and lgbt+ is wrong, and this, in the end, has led to a somewhat toxic culture in many ways, like men who think they can't cry, and girls who are told they can't become engineers. all of this stems from a heteronormative society where we place our gender roles and being straight over being happy and being who you want to be. there are a lot of other ways that the media's influenced lgbt+ culture, and i hope you'll stick around for the rest of them.

butch, femme, and other gender representations for gay's, lesbians and bisexuals.

hollywood has a major issue mainly concerning the representation of lgbt+ people in the media, that being that they're often sloted into small boxes and not allowed to explore much outside of them, it's rare to see a character who isn't fully in one category or another, and this has led to a lot of issues for the lgbt+ community, one of those being 'the question.' it's a question almost every person in a same sex relationship has had to answer. 'so, which one of you is the girl/boy?'

this idea of a 'girl/boy' is a myth that persists in according to many people, about lgbt+ couples. that there has to be a dominate partner in the relationship, but that's just simply untrue. in any relationship what so ever, there never needs to be one person who calls all the shots. however, the media persists with this idea, coding many characters in a same sex relationship with one as the 'female', and one as the 'male.' even in glee, daren criss plays the more 'masculine' partner, even proposing, and the other kid plays the much more 'feminine' partner, squealing over clothing and fashion at almost every given chance. while this isn't necessarily bad, because it gives all the people who do fit into these subgroups a way to look at themselves, it's also harmful because it provides representations of LGBT+ people when a large amount of the population probably doesn't fit into these boxes. so, media affects us a lot. it creates stereotypes that, while maybe there, certainly aren't true for everyone. don't force yourself into these boxes, and keep in mind that you can be whatever you wish.
